Transdown’s Block Structure

Transdown assumes your transcript will be built of blocks, which are just lines of source text separated by one or more blank lines.

Episode titles, turns, and reference lists are all block-level elements. To write in transdown, you should separate those elements (especially turns) from one other with one or more blank lines

These three lines

Are each separated by a blank line

So each line is a block
These three lines
have no blank line in between
and they're all part of the same single block

Transcripts are organized around episodes. You can denote the title of an episode using ATX-style header syntax, which just means beginning a line with one or more # symbols.

Conversational Turns

Conversational Turns are the meat and potatoes of most transcripts. And, when it comes to recognizing what you type, Transdown tries to be really both smart and flexible. The result is that whether you're transcribing with timestamps down to the frame-number, or you're just content-logging and you don't care who's speaking, Transdown just gets it right.
Examples of Valid Turns Explanation 
[36:17] Jazmyn: I think it falls faster because 
it has more mass for gravity to pull.

This turn showcases all the possible markup a turn can have. It's got a timestamp followed by a space ([36:17] ), a speaker-name followed by a colon and a space (Jazmyn: ), and speech. To write turns like this, lead the line with your timestamp (in square brackets), followed by your speaker name, followed by a colon, followed by speech. 

[00:17] Students discuss James A. Garfield's 
novel proof of the Pythagorean Theorem.

Turns don't require a speaker to be named. In this example, we lead a line with a timestamp, which is a [, followed by strings of colon-separated (00:17), followed by ], followed by a space. Using timestamps without speaker-names is a perfect use case for content logging. 

Vader: You are part of the Rebel Alliance 
and a traitor. Take her away!

Turns also don't require a timestamp, as long as a speaker is defined. This turn parses as a speaker name (everything up to the : character), followed by speech

Philosophy Why Transdown Syntax looks like it does

My two main inspirations for Transdown were John Gruber’s Markdown and John August’s Fountain. The inspirational lineage is so strong that I chose the name Transdown to reflect that heritage. And, much of the syntax descends from their ideas about what these languages are and how they should work.

The overriding design goal for Markdown’s formatting syntax is to make it as readable as possible. The idea is that a Markdown-formatted document should be publishable as-is, as plain text, without looking like it’s been marked up with tags or formatting instructions.

John Gruber in Introduction to Markdown

Fountain is not an app. It’s not even really a file format. It’s a simple set of straightforward rules for writing a screenplay in plain text….Fountain is designed to “just work” if you simply typed some text that looked like a screenplay.

John August in Fountain FAQ
